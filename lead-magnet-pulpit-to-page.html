From Pulpit to Page

A short, honest guide for clergy who want to write a book

You have spent years saying things that mattered to the people in front of you. This is a guide to getting one of those things into a book, without losing your voice, your time, or your peace of mind. Read it slowly. There is no rush.

01

Find the one book, not the ten

A lifetime of ministry holds many books. That is the problem, not the gift. The work is choosing one.

  • ☐You can name the single theme that keeps returning in your preaching.
  • ☐You know the one reader you most want this book to reach.
  • ☐You can say, in one sentence, what you want them to carry away.

A book that tries to say everything says nothing. Choose the truth you would preach if you had only one Sunday left.

02

What to do with a drawer of homilies

Homilies are a rich seam, but a stack of them is not yet a book. They were written for the ear and for a single morning. A book is read alone, slowly, and out of season.

  • ☐You have gathered the homilies that circle your one theme.
  • ☐You are willing to let them be reshaped, not merely reprinted.
  • ☐You can see them becoming chapters rather than talks.

The best homily-books are rewritten, not compiled. The voice stays; the form changes.

03

Decide what kind of book it is

Three shapes fit most faith authors. Knowing yours early saves months.

  • ☐Reflections: short pieces on a theme, for prayer and pondering.
  • ☐Memoir: the story of a ministry, a mission, or a season that formed you.
  • ☐Teaching: a clear, warm guide to prayer, grief, discipleship, or the spiritual life.

You do not have to decide alone. But a book that knows what it is finds its reader far more easily.

04

Protect your voice

People will read your book because it sounds like you, not like a textbook. Guard that.

  • ☐You would recognise your own cadence if it were read aloud.
  • ☐Any help you accept works from your words, not over them.
  • ☐You have seen a sample before committing to a whole manuscript.

Editing should improve the writing and never replace the person behind it. If it flattens you, it has failed.

05

Write for one person

You already do this from the pulpit, whether you know it or not. Do it on the page.

  • ☐You can picture one real person who needs this book.
  • ☐You know what keeps that person awake, and where they are hungry.
  • ☐You are writing to them, not to an audience or a market.

Write to one soul and you reach many. Write to everyone and you reach no one.

06

Understand your options for getting it out

You do not need a traditional publisher's permission to make a real book. You do need it made well.

  • ☐You know an ISBN names the publisher of record, and that this can be you, an imprint, or a studio.
  • ☐You understand print-on-demand means the book never goes out of stock.
  • ☐You have considered where your readers actually are: a parish table, a website, Amazon, or all three.

A beautifully made book that only you could have paid for is still a real book. What matters is the care, not the gatekeeper.

07

Money should not be the wall

Many priests and ministers have a book worth making and no budget to fund it. That should not be where the story ends.

  • ☐You know that a no-upfront-cost route exists, where production is shared out of later sales.
  • ☐You have not let a lack of funds quietly close the door on the book.
  • ☐You are willing to ask what is possible before assuming it is not.

Your calling should not be priced out. Ask about the Partnership route before you give up on the idea.

08

Keep what is yours

A good partner never asks you to sign your work away.

  • ☐You understand that the copyright in your words belongs to you.
  • ☐Any agreement leaves the rights and the royalties clearly with you.
  • ☐You could move the book elsewhere later if you ever chose to.

Ownership is not a detail. It is the difference between a book you made and a book that was taken from you.

09

Let the date carry you

A jubilee, an ordination anniversary, or a retirement gives a book its finest reason and its firmest deadline.

  • ☐You have a date that would make the book meaningful to give.
  • ☐You have counted backwards from it to know when to begin.
  • ☐You have told someone, so it feels real and not merely hoped for.

A date turns a someday book into a real one. Begin sooner than feels necessary.

10

A gentle reality check

The priests and ministers who finish are rarely the most gifted writers. They are the ones who kept going.

  • ☐You are ready to write badly at first and fix it later.
  • ☐You will accept help with the parts that are not your calling.
  • ☐You are willing to finish, not only to start.

The book will not be perfect. It only has to be true, and finished.
